I love chocolate chip cookies. I mean - who doesn't? (If you don't, that's odd). I've been making them for years. The first time I made them from scratch by myself was when I was in elementary school, maybe 7 or 8 years old. I remember borrowing a massive kids' baking book from the school library, being by myself in the kitchen, and attempting their chocolate chip cookie recipe. The only issue is, I had not learned fractions just yet. When I read "1 and 3/4 cups of flour," I thought that I had to add the 1 and 3 together... so I added 4 cups of flour. Let's just say that they were veryyyy floury cookies.

I've come a long way since then. Chocolate chip cookies are really easy for me to make and feel like second nature. I discovered Bon Appétit's "Ultimate Chocolate Chip Cookie Recipe" by Chris Morocco around when COVID hit in 2020. These brought my first experience ever browning butter and were so easy and dependable that they quickly became my go-to recipe. I love these cookies, but are they my ultimate recipe for chocolate chip cookies? I'm not so sure. So, what am I to do? It's time for more recipe testing. This time, I'm diving more into the science of it all. I used to NOT be into the science of it AT ALL! But recently it's been becoming more interesting. The science begins to make sense and gives a meaning and backing to the decisions made for the cookies.

I really want to test this again and again and get it right for myself. I know that the ultimate cookie for me is not going to be the same as any other blogger or baker on the internet, and that is totally okay. So come along for my Recipe Test #1 of chocolate chip cookies!

## Choosing Between Methods

From my research, there are 3 main methods for bringing chocolate chip cookies together; specifically methods to create the sugar-lipid-protein structure of a cookie.

**Method 1: Creaming**

This method is the usual, traditional way to make chocolate chip cookies, especially for bakeries. The sugar and room temperature butter are creamed together, and the chemistry/physics is actually pretty cool. The sugar aerates the butter by carving small air pockets into the fat. When these air pockets are heated in the oven, they expand! This makes a soft/pillowy cookie that comes out of the oven but due to the unstableness of the emulsion, the cookie can get hard pretty soon after being baked.

**Method 2: Egg Foaming**

In this method, the sugar is whisked with the eggs before liquid butter is intoduced to the mix. This creates a stable protein structure of the eggs and sugar that traps small air bubbles. An emulsion is formed when the butter is streamed in. The two cool things about this method were that the cookie has less grease spillage. Because the proteins are pre-stablized in this way, there isn't as much fat leaking out of the final cookies. Also, you get a shiny crust on top and a thicker, uniform, somewhat cakey cookie.

**Method 3: Toffee**

This method involves whisking sugar into lukewarm browned butter, followed by the eggs. This method creates a kind of loose partial caramel, as the sugar partially dissolves into the brown butter. The result of this is a cookie that is dense, chewy, very brown-buttery, and butterstochy.
